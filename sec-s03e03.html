    Section 3.3 Episode 3: Multiplying Fractions

    Episode 3 Learning Goals.

    • Multiply fractions
      🔗
      🔗
    • “Pre-simplify” the result of a fraction multiplication through “cross-cancelling.”
      🔗
      🔗
    • Raise fractions to powers.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Episode 3 Key Terminology.

    • Cross-cancel
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Fractions can be multiplied via the following process:
    1. Multiply the two numerators.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Multiply the two denominators.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Simplify the result.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.3.1.

    Multiply the fractions:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{2}{3}\times \frac{9}{10} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution: We multiply the numerators together, and we multiply the denominators together:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{2\times 9}{3\times 10} = \frac{18}{30} \end{equation*}
    Then, we simplify the result. Here, we notice that both 18 and 30 can be divided by 3:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{18\div 3}{30\div 3} = \frac{6}{10} \end{equation*}
    We also notice that 6 and 10 can be divided by 2:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{6\div 2}{10\div 2} = \frac{3}{5} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Makes Sense, Right?

    Remember from Episode 2 that the word “of” corresponds to multiplication. So half of half of a pizza would correspond to:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{2}\times \frac{1}{2} \end{equation*}
    Physically, half of half of a pizza is one-quarter of a pizza:
    🔗
    Mathematically,
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{2}\times\frac{1}{2} = \frac{1\times 1}{2\times 2} = \frac{1}{4} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Notice that dividing by 1 has no effect on a number:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3}{1} = 3 \end{equation*}
    So, if you need to multiply a fraction by a whole number, put the whole number over a denominator of 1.
    🔗

    Example 3.3.2.

    Calculate the product:
    \begin{equation*} 6\times \frac{1}{2} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution: Put the whole number, 6, over a denominator of 1:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{6}{1}\times \frac{1}{2} \end{equation*}
    Multiply the numerators and denominators:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{6\times 1}{1\times 2} = \frac{6}{2} \end{equation*}
    Then, simplify:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{6}{2} = 3 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Makes Sense, Right?

    If I have 6 objects, and I want half of those 6 objects, then half of those 6 objects is 3 objects.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.3.3.

    Calculate the value of
    \begin{equation*} \left(\frac{2}{2}\right)^4 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Remember that raising a number to the fourth power means multiplying together 4 copies of the number:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{2}{3}\times\frac{2}{3}\times\frac{2}{3}\times\frac{2}{3} \end{equation*}
    We multiply the numerators and denominators:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{2\times 2\times 2\times 2}{3\times 3\times 3\times 3} = \frac{16}{81} \end{equation*}
    This cannot be simplified any further, so 16/81 is our final answer.
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 3.3.4.

    Cross-canceling: When multiplying fractions, you can simplify your answer before multiplying the numerator and denominator.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.3.5.

    Multiply these fractions, using cross-cancelling:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{2}{3}\times \frac{9}{10} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution: Notice that the number 2 divides the first numerator and second denominator, so divide each of those by 2:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{2\div 2}{3}\times \frac{9}{10\div 2} = \frac{1}{3}\times \frac{9}{5} \end{equation*}
    Notice that the number 3 divides the second numerator and the first denominator, so divide each of those by 3:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{3\div 3}\times \frac{9\div 3}{5} = \frac{1}{1}\times\frac{3}{5} \end{equation*}
    Now we finish the multiplication:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1\times 3}{1\times 5} = \frac{3}{5} \end{equation*}
    And we notice that the final answer is already fully simplified!
    🔗
    🔗

    Be Careful.

    This “cross-cancelling” trick only works when multiplying fractions. It won’t work when adding, subtracting, or dividing fractions.
    🔗
    🔗
    When working with negative fractions, use the same rules for multiplying negative numbers that we learned back in Season 2.
    🔗

    Example 3.3.6.

    Multiply:
    \begin{equation*} \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)\times\left(-\frac{4}{5}\right) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution: We start by counting the number of minus signs in the problem. There is one minus sign. Then, we multiply the numbers as if they were both positive:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{2}\times\frac{4}{5} \end{equation*}
    Cross-cancel the 2s in the 2 and in the 4:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{1}\times\frac{2}{5} \end{equation*}
    Then multiply the numerators and multiply the denominators:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1\times 2}{1\times 5} = \frac{2}{5} \end{equation*}
    Remember that the original problem had one minus sign, an odd number. So, that means the final answer must be negative:
    \begin{equation*} -\frac{2}{5} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
