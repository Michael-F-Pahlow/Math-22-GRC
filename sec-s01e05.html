    Section 1.5 Episode 1: Median and Mean

    Episode 5 Learning Goals.

    • Informally describe the meaning of mean / average.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Compare / contrast the median and the average.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Calculate the mean / average of a list of numbers.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Calculate the median of a list of numbers.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Episode 5 Key Terminology.

    • Mean (a.k.a. "average")
      🔗
      🔗
    • Median
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 1.5.1.

    The Mean, also called the average, is a number that we can calculate that will tell us the cost of a “typical” or “normal” house on this street.
    🔗
    To calculate the average of a list of numbers:
    1. Count how many numbers you have in the list.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Add all the numbers in the list together.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Divide the sum from Step 2 by the count from Step 1.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.5.2.

    Suppose you’re looking at the prices of all houses on a certain street:
    • $650,000
      🔗
      🔗
    • $625,000
      🔗
      🔗
    • $415,000
      🔗
      🔗
    • $600,000
      🔗
      🔗
    • $680,000
      🔗
      🔗
    • $790,000
      🔗
      🔗
    • $750,000
      🔗
      🔗
    • $670,000
      🔗
      🔗
    Find the average of the house prices listed above.
    🔗
    Solution: First, we count the number of house prices in the list. We have 8 house prices in total.
    🔗
    Then, we add all the house prices together. Using a calculator, we get:
    \begin{equation*} 650,000 + 625,000 + 415,000 + 600,000 + 680,000 + 790,000 + 750,000 + 670,000 = 5,180,000 \end{equation*}
    Then, we divide:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{5,180,00}{8} = 647,500 \end{equation*}
    So, the average price of a house on that street is $647,500.
    🔗
    Although some houses may be cheaper and some may be more expensive, a typical, normal house on that street should cost around $647,500.
    🔗
    🔗
    If one of the numbers in your list is an outlier (much larger or smaller than the other number), that can throw off the average, making it useless.
    🔗

    Example 1.5.3.

    The annual salaries of 6 employees were recorded:
    • $81,000
      🔗
      🔗
    • $89,000
      🔗
      🔗
    • $97,000
      🔗
      🔗
    • $81,000
      🔗
      🔗
    • $92,000
      🔗
      🔗
    • $1,00,000,000 (CEO)
      🔗
      🔗
    What’s the average salary?
    🔗
    Solution: The number of salaries we’ve recorded is 6.
    🔗
    The sum of the salaries is:
    \begin{equation*} 81,000 + 89,000 + 97,000 + 81,000 + 92,000 + 1,000,000,000 =1,000,440,000 \end{equation*}
    Now we divide:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1,000,440,000}{6} = 166,740,000 \end{equation*}
    So the average salary is $166,740,500.
    🔗
    This average is useless for telling us anything about the “typical” salary at this company -- it’s much too small to reflect the CEO’s salary, and it’s much too big to reflect everyone else’s salary. The average is useless here because the CEO’s salary is an outlier.
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 1.5.4.

    The median is another number that tells us what’s “normal” or “typical.”
    🔗
    To calculate the median of a list of numbers:
    1. Sort the number, smallest to largest
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The number in the middle of the list is the median.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    The median is better than the average when there are outliers, because the median is not thrown off by outliers. The outliers are always the highest or lowest values but the median is always the middle number.
    🔗

    Example 1.5.5.

    Seven students took a quiz and got the following scores:
    • 90
      🔗
      🔗
    • 98
      🔗
      🔗
    • 95
      🔗
      🔗
    • 92
      🔗
      🔗
    • 93
      🔗
      🔗
    • 0
      🔗
      🔗
    • 87
      🔗
      🔗
    Calculate the median score.
    🔗
    Solution: First, we put the numbers in order, smallest to largest:
    \begin{equation*} 0, 87, 90, 92, 93, 95, 98 \end{equation*}
    The number in the middle of the list, 92, is the median.
    🔗
    Notice that, even though there’s an outlier in the list (the 0, which is much less than the other scores), the median of 92 is still a good representation of how the typical student did on the quiz.
    🔗
    🔗
    If your list has an even count (e.g. if there are eight numbers in your list, or if there are ten numbers in your list), there won’t be a single number in the middle of the list.
    🔗
    In this situation, find the median by averaging the two numbers in the middle of the list.
    🔗

    Example 1.5.6.

    The annual salaries of 6 employees were recorded:
    • $81,000
      🔗
      🔗
    • $89,000
      🔗
      🔗
    • $97,000
      🔗
      🔗
    • $81,000
      🔗
      🔗
    • $92,000
      🔗
      🔗
    • $1,00,000,000 (CEO)
      🔗
      🔗
    What’s the median salary?
    🔗
    Solution: First, we put the salaries in order, smallest to largest:
    \begin{equation*} 81000, 81000, 89000, 92000, 97000, 1000000000 \end{equation*}
    Because we have an even count of salaries (six), there are two numbers in the middle of the list, 89000 and 92000. So, to find the median, we average these numbers.
    🔗
    To average two numbers, we add them and divide the result by 2:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{89000+90500}{2} = 90,500 \end{equation*}
    So, the median salary is $90,500.
    🔗
    This median essentially ignores the CEO’s giant outlier salary, and it instead gives you a value that’s typical for the other employees.
    🔗
    🔗
